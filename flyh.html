b a c k g r o u n d

Based on personal interest, I joined a group of MBA students working on a start-up idea as a design consultant to help guide and launch a product for East Asian advanced English learners. Since most existing English-learning tools are geared toward beginners and intermediates, we saw a market opportunity for a tool for advanced English learners. I worked on this project from end to end, leading the user research and designing the final MVP.

t h e    p r o b l e m

no easy way for East Asians to study & practice English, especially for advanced learners

Learning a new language is hard. Getting to advanced/native proficiency is even harder - languages have nuances and are culturally charged. Exisiting tools and services for English learning in East Asia are limited and insufficient, especially for advanced-level learners. Tools like Duolingo and Rosetta Stone are geared toward beginners and intermediates, and tutors and English classes take too much time. How can we help East Asian advanced English learners improve their English skills more efficiently?

We gained confirmation that current tools are insufficient as over 50% either self-teach or do not use any tools/services.

ITERATIONS

fail fast, iterate fast - accept when users debunk your hypothesis

We were so excited about our first product idea and thought that advanced English learners would love our Coffee Meets Bagel-style idea. However, upon testing with 6 users and pitching to 2 VCs, we found that our users:

  • liked the idea of the app, but probably wouldn't use it
  • didn't want "lecture-based" study materials
  • didn't want to download a separate mobile app

Another key finding was that beginner-level learners or those who are specifically studying to pass an English exam (e.g., TOEFL for study abroad) would use "lecture-based" materials, but advanced speakers don't want to spend extra time learning English - they want to have fun during the process.

attempt #2: shifting to an experience-centric approach

Our feedback led us to focus more on experiences and strategies to gain users. After a second round of ideation, our top idea was a Facebook plugin that curates "learning experiences" based on interests and location. Users can try out a class for free, then later buy package deals. I had really high hopes for this idea since "English Cafes" (a native speaker facilitates casual English conversations with a group of learners) are so popular in Japan.

design with the user's culture in mind

Sadly, our idea was shut down by our users again - all 6 users that we tested with responded negatively. The main complaints were that going out to learn English was too bothersome and they were afraid of embarrassing themselves by speaking English with strangers.

mix & match to create a good balance

From our first concept, users loved the idea of video curation based on interest and difficulty level. since it's fun and not "study-based". In addition, we found an opportunity to utilize existing platforms and content like Facebook, YouTube, and Netflix. Our MBA team met with over 20 VC teams and investors in Silicon Valley, some of which were very interested in the idea of bringing American content to East Asian countries for English learning.

t h e    s o l u t i o n

Fly High is a mobile application that provides advanced English learners in East Asia with personalized video content relevant to their interests and proficiency-level with built-in language learning. This allows users to learn English through cultural immersion rather than traditional studying.
